When the Regulator Becomes the Evangelist

Three things that caught my attention this week at the intersection of AI, governance, and financial services.

🌏
US Treasury Policy Shift

The US Treasury just reframed AI governance entirely — "failure to adopt is its own risk."

The AI Innovation Series launched last week isn't just another consultation. Treasury Secretary explicitly stated they're moving from "constraint" toward recognizing that "failure to adopt productivity-enhancing technology as its own risk." Let that sink in.

The financial stability regulator is saying AI non-adoption threatens stability. This is the cloud playbook accelerated — remember when APRA asked "why cloud?" then pivoted to "why NOT cloud?" AI governance is on that same arc, just faster.

If you're still treating AI as an innovation experiment rather than core infrastructure, you're already behind the regulatory expectation curve.

Sources:

  • Treasury AI Innovation Series launch
🏙️
Singapore MAS Framework

Singapore just dropped an AI Risk Management Toolkit — and it's deliberately framework-agnostic.

MAS's new toolkit covers traditional AI, GenAI, and "emerging AI technologies" without prescribing specific controls. Smart move — technology-neutral governance that won't be obsolete in 18 months.

The parallel to notice: while the EU AI Act chases specific compliance deadlines (August 2026 high-risk systems), Singapore and the US are converging on principles-based, outcomes-focused approaches.

My prediction: APRA follows the Singapore/US path, not Brussels. Build governance that assumes continuous evolution, not checkbox compliance to a fixed standard.

🔧
FINRA Risk Warning

FINRA's 2026 priorities flag a gap most small firms haven't seen coming: agentic AI governance.

The regulator specifically called out that autonomous systems "create novel risks that small firms are often ill-equipped to manage." This isn't theoretical — agentic AI with Non-Human Identities (NHIs) can now execute trades, move funds, sign contracts.

Your identity and access management framework probably assumes humans behind every action. At 100+ agents per employee, that assumption breaks.

Start with NHI lifecycle management now, before your next regulatory exam includes questions you can't answer.
